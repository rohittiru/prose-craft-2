# Human Signal

How to execute the divergent poles of the Posture Board without performing them. Each dial below is a measured difference between human-authored and AI fiction (StoryScope, Russell et al. 2026, ~61k stories, narrative structure with style stripped out). The percentages are population averages, not laws — your job is not to hit a number, it's to break Claude's habit of landing on the same pole every time.

**Read this whole file as dials, never commandments.** The failure mode this skill is built to avoid is *convergence* — every piece in the same place. If you turn every dial to its human pole on every piece, you have simply built a new machine center. Per piece, choose; across pieces, vary.

---

## Dial 1 — Time order: linear → nonlinear

**The data.** Humans use chronological discontinuity, flashback/flash-forward, and nonlinear framing markedly more than AI. They more often stage a revelation that forces you to *reread* earlier scenes in a new light (recontextualization). AI defaults to first-clue-to-final-reveal, straight through.

**Reach for it when** the story has a buried cause, a secret, a "before," or a reveal whose power depends on the reader having already passed the evidence without noticing. A mystery that opens at the funeral and spirals backward through decades is doing what AI rarely does.

**How to execute without performing:**
- Don't announce the jumps ("Three years earlier…" as a crutch). Let a smell, an object, a line of dialogue trigger the slip in time.
- Plant the detail that the later reveal will re-light. The reader should feel the structure pay off, not see the scaffolding.
- A single well-placed flash-forward or a withheld first scene can do it. You don't need a fractured timeline throughout.

**When NOT to:** a genuine real-time unity-of-action piece (a single tense hour) is *stronger* linear. Nonlinearity bolted onto it is just noise. Choose this dial for the material, not to tick the box.

## Dial 2 — Emotion rendering: embodied-only → sometimes named

**The data.** This is the single largest gap. AI conveys emotion through the body and physical metaphor 81% of the time vs 38% for humans — the "tightening chest, cold sweat, dimming light." Humans *name* emotions outright 29% of the time vs just 8% for AI. The original prose-craft's flagship rule ("never write 'she was angry'") pushes Claude harder toward the machine pole.

**Reach for naming when:**
- The embodied version would just be more sensory fog. "She was afraid. Plainly, stupidly afraid." can hit harder than another paragraph of somatic detail.
- The voice is plain, blunt, or grief-flattened — grief simplifies language, and "She was not all right. She would not be all right for a long time" is more human than a catalogue of body sensations.
- You've already rendered two emotions through the body in this piece. The third named flatly is variance.

**How to keep it from going amateur:** naming an emotion is not the same as *explaining* it. "She was furious, because the meeting had gone badly and she felt disrespected" is telling-plus-justifying — still bad. "She was furious. She drove home anyway." is naming, then moving. Name and leave it.

**When NOT to:** a moment built entirely on subtext, where the whole point is the gap between surface and feeling, should stay unnamed. Don't name the thing the scene exists to imply.

## Dial 3 — Narrator ↔ reader: sealed → direct address

**The data.** Humans break the fourth wall far more (67% vs 39%) and directly address the reader far more (28% vs 7%). AI writes "as though no one is watching." **But note:** this is a population *rate*, not a per-piece prescription. The point is that it should be a live option that shows up across your pieces — not that any one piece should wink constantly.

**Reach for it when** the narrator has a personality — a storyteller, a confessor, an unreliable charmer, an essayist's "I." An aside to "you, dear reader," a narrator who admits they're choosing what to tell you, a confession that you're being lied to.

**How to execute as a real choice, not a tic:**
- Commit at the posture level: this piece *has* a narrator who knows you're there, or it doesn't. Don't sprinkle one stray aside into an otherwise sealed dream.
- Make the address do work — intimacy, complicity, irony, menace — not decoration.
- One strong fourth-wall moment can define a voice. Frequency is not the goal; *deliberateness* is.

**When NOT to:** a deep-immersion, close-third, dream-state piece is *destroyed* by the narrator tapping the glass. If the effect is total absorption, keep the dream sealed — and let *other* pieces carry this dial.

**This is the dial the user specifically flagged: it must read as a style choice, deployed variably across work, never as a new default.**

## Dial 4 — World reference: self-contained → names the real

**The data.** Humans reference specific named texts, authors, and the real world at nearly double the AI rate (47% vs 24%) and mix explicit and implicit reference more evenly. AI avoids naming real brands, places, songs, and works, retreating into vague allusion ("an old song," "a famous painting").

**Reach for it when** specificity grounds the world. "She played Nina Simone" beats "she played some old jazz record." A real street, a real brand of cigarette, a real book on the nightstand — these locate the story in the shared world rather than a generic dreamscape.

**How to execute without performing:**
- The reference must be load-bearing — character, era, class, mood — not a sprinkle of brand names to look knowing.
- Right and specific beats famous. The particular regional soda, the out-of-print book, the local band — specificity is the signal, not celebrity.

**Caveat (weight this dial moderately):** the human baseline is allusion-dense published literary fiction, so part of this gap may be anthology bias. Use real-world reference where it earns its place; don't cram a reading list into a story that doesn't want one.

**When NOT to:** fable, myth, deliberately placeless or timeless work. Some stories want the generic on purpose. That's a posture too — just don't let it be your *only* posture.

## Dial 5 — Escalation: flat/quiet → genuine rise

**The data.** Claude's #1 fingerprint: weaker event escalation than any other source, and the lowest event-type diversity. Claude favors epilogues and quiet endings over "avalanche" endings. The original prose-craft's "restraint is power / quiet earned ending" canonizes exactly this fingerprint.

**Reach for real escalation when** the story has stakes that should compound. Let the second event be bigger than the first and the third bigger than the second (auxesis/climax). Let an ending *bang* — a confrontation that actually detonates, a consequence that lands hard, an avalanche rather than a fade.

**How to execute:**
- Vary event *type*, not just intensity — a death, then a betrayal, then a discovery, not three variations on melancholy.
- Permit the loud ending. Not every close should be a character sitting quietly with a realization. Sometimes the house should burn down.
- Rising sentence length and rhythm can carry the build (see `sound-and-rhythm.md`, `pacing-and-paragraphs.md`).

**When NOT to:** a true miniature — a single quiet moment — is supposed to stay quiet. The point is not "always escalate." The point is that the quiet fade should be *one* of your endings, not all of them.

## Dial 6 — Plot shape: single-track → multiplicity

**The data.** AI strongly favors one tidy causal track: "no subplots" 79% vs 57%, protagonist-driven resolution 69% vs 46%, resolution via internal acceptance 47% vs 27%. Humans run more subplots (and tie them back to theme), inhabit more locations, carry more dialogue relative to narration, and let external events — not just the protagonist's growth — drive resolution.

**Reach for it when** the story can hold more than one thread. A subplot that rhymes with or complicates the main line. A second location that changes the register. A resolution that comes partly from outside the protagonist's control rather than purely from their internal arc.

**How to execute without sprawl:**
- Subplots should *echo* the central theme (thematically parallel or pointedly contrasting), not wander off. Integration is the human move, not mere quantity.
- Let the world act on the protagonist. Coincidence, other people's choices, external force — resolution doesn't have to be earned solely through the hero's epiphany.

**Caveat (weight this dial softly):** this is the cluster most likely to be an artifact of the study's setup (AI wrote to a brief with a fixed word count, as a cover version). So treat multiplicity as a real option but don't force subplots into a piece that is genuinely a single clean line.

**When NOT to:** flash fiction and tight single-scene pieces want one track. Don't break a miniature by stuffing subplots in.

## Dial 7 — Protagonist morality: clear → ambivalent

**The data.** Humans present morally ambivalent/mixed protagonists 59% of the time vs 38% for AI. AI leans toward clearly sympathetic, clearly-good-or-redeemed protagonists.

**Reach for it when** the character can be genuinely hard to root for — selfish in a way that isn't cute, cruel without full justification, complicit, self-deceiving. Let the reader's sympathy be *uneasy* rather than secured.

**How to execute:**
- Don't redeem on schedule. AI tends to deliver the growth/acceptance arc. A protagonist who ends roughly as compromised as they began is a human move.
- Withhold the narrator's verdict. Don't have the prose (or other characters) reassure the reader that the protagonist is, deep down, good.

**When NOT to:** some stories want moral clarity — a child's POV, a fable, certain comedy. Clarity is a legitimate choice; just don't let *clean and sympathetic* be the only protagonist you ever write.

## Dial 8 — Sensory density: lush → spare

**The data.** AI over-writes the senses: embodied emotion, setting-as-psychological-mirror, and heavy smell imagery (olfactory 82% vs 57%), high overall sensory density. The original prose-craft tells nearly every effect to "layer the senses" — a one-directional dial.

**Reach for restraint when** plain statement would hit harder than description. A spare, under-described scene — a few flat declaratives, the senses withheld — can land like a blow precisely because it refuses to lavish. Grief, shock, and dissociation often want *less* sensory detail, not more.

**How to execute:**
- Cut the third sensory clause. One precise image beats three.
- Especially watch the smell reflex and the setting-mirrors-the-mood reflex — both are strong AI tells. If the weather is matching the protagonist's heart, consider letting it not.
- Let some scenes be nearly sense-free — pure action or pure speech.

**When NOT to:** a deliberately lush, lyrical, immersive passage *should* be rich. Beauty is still a legitimate aim (see `effects-toolkit.md`). The fix is not "always spare" — it's that lushness should be a chosen register, not the automatic setting.

## Dial 9 — Dialogue function: single → mixed

**The data.** AI dialogue serves *philosophical debate* far more than human dialogue (59% vs 34%) — characters who monologue ideas at each other. Human dialogue does more varied work: gossip and rumor (a notable human/GPT move), logistics, evasion, comedy, power play, small talk that hides a knife.

**Reach for variety when** a scene's talk is turning into a seminar. Let characters gossip, negotiate, lie, misunderstand, talk past each other, discuss something trivial while the real thing goes unsaid.

**How to execute:**
- Default dialogue to subtext and friction, not thesis statements. People rarely say the true thing directly (see `dialogue-and-voice.md`).
- Let at least some conversations be about nothing important on the surface — the human texture is in the indirection.
- Reserve the explicitly philosophical exchange for when the story has truly earned it, and even then, undercut it.

**When NOT to:** a story whose subject genuinely is ideas (a debate, a confession, a deathbed argument) can carry weighty talk. Just make sure that's a choice, not the only thing your characters ever do.

---

## Using the dials together

You do not turn all nine to "human." You take the Posture Board, push **at least three** off the default pole *for reasons that fit this piece*, commit to them, and write. The other six can sit at whatever the material wants — including the default pole, when the default genuinely serves the story.

Across many pieces, because you start from the same fixed center every time and deliberately push off it, the body of work spreads out. That spread — not any single divergent move — is the human signal.
