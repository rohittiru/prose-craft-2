# Discourse & Argument Devices

How ideas are introduced, defended, emphasized, or undermined at the sentence and paragraph level. These devices originated in rhetoric and oratory but work equally well in essays, articles, and persuasive prose. Some appear in fiction through characters who argue, persuade, or manipulate.

---

## Introducing Ideas

**Hypophora** — Asking a question and immediately answering it. "What is honor? A word. What is in that word honor? Air." Controls the reader's attention by raising and resolving questions in sequence. Creates a sense of thoroughness, of every angle being covered.

**Procatalepsis** — Anticipating an objection and answering it before it's raised. "You'll say this is too expensive. But consider what it costs to do nothing." Pre-empts resistance. Creates the impression of a mind that has already thought further than the reader has.

**Rhetorical question** — A question asked for effect, not answer. "Who among us has not felt this?" The reader answers internally, and in doing so agrees with the writer. Use sparingly — too many rhetorical questions feel like badgering.

---

## Emphasis and Persuasion

**Amplification** — Repeating a point while adding detail each time. Not just repetition — expansion. Each pass adds a new dimension. "It was bad. It was bad in the way that only government projects can be bad — expensively, thoroughly, and with an audit trail."

**Apophasis / Paralipsis** — Bringing something up by denying you'll bring it up. "I won't even mention his criminal record." The denial IS the mention. Devastatingly effective in argumentation and political speech. In fiction, reveals a character who manipulates.

**Innuendo** — Implying without stating. The meaning is deniable but unmistakable. "I noticed all the bottles in your liquor cabinet are empty." The accusation lives in the gap between what's said and what's meant.

**Aporia** — The rhetorical expression of doubt or puzzlement. "To be or not to be, that is the question." Creates the appearance of thinking in real time, weighing options genuinely. Makes the speaker seem honest rather than rehearsed.

---

## Softening and Redirecting

**Antanagoge** — Placing a positive next to a negative to reduce its impact. "The food was terrible, but the portions were enormous." In sincere use: "We may be struggling now, but we've survived worse." The positive doesn't erase the negative — it reframes it.

**Metanoia** — Correcting yourself mid-statement. "All faults that may be named — nay, that hell knows." The self-correction creates emphasis — the revised version feels more true because it replaced something less true.

**Accismus** — Feigning disinterest in something you want. "Oh, I suppose if no one else wants the last piece..." Reveals character. Useful in fiction for portraying vanity, false modesty, or social gamesmanship.

---

## Argument by Omission

**Enthymeme** — A syllogism with the premise omitted. The reader fills the gap, which makes them complicit in the argument. "They say it takes hundreds of years to build a nation. Welcome to Singapore." The missing premise (Singapore is young) is supplied by the reader, making the conclusion feel like the reader's own discovery.

---

## Usage Notes for Fiction

These devices appear most naturally in:
- Characters who are lawyers, politicians, salespeople, clergy, teachers, manipulators
- First-person narrators who are trying to convince the reader of something
- Unreliable narrators who deploy rhetoric to obscure the truth
- Essayistic passages within fiction — the narrator reflecting, arguing, philosophizing
- Dialogue where one character is trying to persuade, seduce, or dominate another

They rarely belong in third-person narration of action or scene unless the narrator has a strong argumentative voice.
