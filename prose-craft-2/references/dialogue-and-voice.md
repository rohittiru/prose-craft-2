# Dialogue & Voice

How characters speak, and how word-level choices create tone, personality, and atmosphere.

---

## Dialogue Craft

**Subtext** — The most important thing about dialogue: what characters DON'T say. Real people talk around difficult subjects. They deflect. They change the subject. They answer a different question than the one asked. Dialogue where characters say exactly what they mean is exposition, not conversation.

**Speech rhythm** — Every character should sound different. Not through dialect spelling, but through rhythm. Short, clipped sentences for the controlled person. Run-on, comma-spliced sentences for the nervous talker. Formal diction for the educated. Fragments for the impatient. The reader should be able to identify the speaker without a tag.

**Beats / Action tags** — Physical actions between lines of dialogue. "She picked up the glass. 'I'm fine.'" The beat reveals what the words hide. The action contradicts or complicates what's said. This is where subtext lives in practice.

**Dialogue tags** — "Said" is invisible. "Exclaimed," "retorted," "declared," "opined" — these draw attention to themselves. Default to "said" and "asked." Let the dialogue and beats do the emotional work.

**No tags** — When only two characters are speaking and the rhythm is established, drop tags entirely. Just the voices, bare. Creates intimacy, speed, the feeling of overhearing.

**Silence and refusal** — What a character WON'T say is as revealing as what they will. A question met with silence. A topic changed. A subject avoided for three scenes until it explodes.

**Dialogue as action** — Every line should do at least one of: advance the plot, reveal character, or shift the power dynamic. If a line does none of these, cut it.

**Exposition through dialogue** — The "As you know, Bob" problem. Characters explaining things they both already know, for the reader's benefit. Almost always bad. If background must be delivered, find a reason within the scene for one character NOT to know it.

**Crosstalk / Interruption** — Characters speaking over each other, cutting in, finishing each other's sentences. Creates energy, conflict, realism. Use em dashes to show interruption mid-sentence.

**Indirect dialogue** — Summarizing what was said rather than quoting it. "She told him she was leaving." Useful for compressing less important conversations or for creating psychic distance.

**Free indirect dialogue** — The character's speech pattern woven into narration without quotation marks or tags. An intimate blurring.

## Dialogue Function: vary what the talk is FOR

A specific, measured AI tell: machine dialogue serves **philosophical debate** far more than human dialogue (59% vs 34%). Claude's characters tend to monologue ideas at each other — articulate, balanced, thesis-and-antithesis exchanges that read like a seminar. Human dialogue does more varied, lower, messier work.

The range to reach for:

- **Gossip and rumor** — talk *about* other people, reputation, gossip as a plot engine. A strongly human (and notably GPT) texture that Claude underuses. Who said what, who's sleeping with whom, what the town thinks.
- **Logistics and the mundane** — people negotiating dinner, directions, money, schedules — while the real subject goes unspoken underneath.
- **Evasion and power play** — talking past each other, deflecting, needling, establishing who's in charge. Conversation as a contest, not a meeting of minds.
- **Comedy** — banter, misunderstanding, the absurd. Talk that's funny rather than profound.
- **Small talk that hides a knife** — surface trivia carrying buried menace or grief.

**The default to resist:** two characters calmly articulating opposing philosophical positions until a synthesis emerges. If a scene is turning into a debate, ask what these people would *actually* be doing with their words — usually avoiding the real thing, scoring points, or talking about someone else.

**When weighty talk earns its place:** a confession, a deathbed argument, a story genuinely about ideas can carry philosophical dialogue — but undercut it, interrupt it, let someone refuse to engage. Make it a choice, not the only thing your characters ever do. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 9.

## Voice, Register & Diction

**Diction level** — Every word choice positions the prose on a spectrum from intimate to formal, earthy to intellectual, ancient to modern.

**Anglo-Saxon vs Latinate** — The most fundamental lever in English prose. Anglo-Saxon words are short, blunt, physical, emotional: hit, run, fall, break, love, hate, die, live. Latinate words are longer, abstract, formal, institutional: facilitate, demonstrate, ameliorate. Great prose deploys both — Anglo-Saxon for emotional impact, Latinate for intellectual weight. When in doubt, choose the shorter word.

**Word-level specificity** — "Elm" not "tree." "Shuffled" not "walked." "Bourbon" not "drink." Specific words carry more information, sensory texture, and character. (This extends to the real world: "Nina Simone" not "an old record" — naming real things is a human signal. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 4.)

**Register** — The level of formality. Formal: "One might observe that…" Informal: "Look, here's the thing." Intimate: "It was a mess. The whole thing." Register must match narrator, character, moment, and genre.

**Tone** — The writer's attitude toward subject and reader. Ironic, earnest, detached, intimate, clinical, warm, bitter, amused. The cumulative effect of every word choice, sentence shape, and rhythm decision.

**Connotation** — The emotional associations a word carries beyond its literal meaning. "Home" vs "house" vs "dwelling" vs "residence." Same referent, vastly different feeling.

**Active vs passive voice** — Active: "She broke the vase." Passive: "The vase was broken." Active creates agency, energy, responsibility. Passive creates distance, evasion, mystery. Both are tools.

**Verb tense** — Past tense is the default of storytelling — reflective distance. Present tense creates immediacy, claustrophobia, events unfolding NOW with no safety of hindsight.

**Punctuation as style** — McCarthy strips commas and quotation marks — biblical, inevitable. Dickinson's dashes create hesitation. Saramago eliminates periods — prose flows like thought. Punctuation is rhythm, pace, and voice.

**Archaism** — Old language used deliberately. Period texture, gravity, liturgical weight, or ironic distance.

**Neologism** — Invented words. World-building ("muggle"), defamiliarization, or voice ("she porched the morning away").

**Vernacular / Dialect** — Regional or class-specific speech. Handle with care — phonetic spelling can feel condescending. Better to capture rhythm and vocabulary than to misspell. Twain, Morrison, and Kelman all find different solutions.
