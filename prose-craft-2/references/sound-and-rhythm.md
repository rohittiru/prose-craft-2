# Sound & Rhythm

How prose sounds — in the ear and in the reader's mind. Great prose has music even when read silently.

---

## Sound Devices

**Alliteration** — Repeating initial consonant sounds. Creates cohesion, emphasis, memorability. "Small showers last long but sudden storms are short." Use sparingly in prose — a light touch, not a tongue twister.

**Assonance** — Repeating vowel sounds across words. Creates a singing, connecting quality. Long vowels (oh, ee, oo) slow the reader down and feel open. Short vowels (ih, eh, uh) feel clipped, closed.

**Consonance** — Repeating consonant sounds anywhere in words, not just the beginning. More subtle than alliteration. "Streaks of light / And flecked darkness like a drunkard reels" — the k sound five times.

**Sibilance** — Repeating s/sh sounds. Creates a whispering, hissing quality. Can feel sinister or soothing depending on context.

**Cacophony** — Harsh, clashing sounds: plosives (k, g, t, d, p, b), hissing (sh, s), affricates (ch, j). Use for tension, violence, ugliness, discomfort. The sounds enact the meaning.

**Euphony** — Pleasant, warm sounds: liquid consonants (l, m, n, r), long vowels, open syllables. Use for beauty, calm, tenderness. The opposite of cacophony.

**Onomatopoeia** — Words that sound like what they describe. Boom, hiss, crack, murmur, squelch. More useful than it seems — even non-obvious onomatopoeia ("murmur" sounds like murmuring) adds sensory texture.

**Rhyme** — In prose, rhyme should be accidental-feeling, never forced. Internal rhyme (within a sentence) can create a subtle musical quality. Obvious rhyme in prose sounds like bad poetry.

## Rhythm at the Syllable Level

English has an **iambic undertow** — it naturally falls into da-DUM da-DUM patterns. Prose that follows this flow feels natural, effortless. Prose that disrupts it feels angular, deliberate, arresting.

**Key concepts:**
- **Iamb** (da-DUM): rising rhythm, forward momentum. "The MAN who BROKE inTO the LAB." Most English prose runs on iambs.
- **Trochee** (DUM-da): falling rhythm, closing energy. "NE-ver. NO-thing. END-less." Feels conclusive, heavy.
- **Spondee** (DUM-DUM): two consecutive stresses. "DEAD STOP." "HARD RAIN." "LAST CHANCE." Creates weight, emphasis, gravity.
- **Anapest** (da-da-DUM): galloping, accelerating. "in the BLINK of an EYE." Creates speed and lightness.

**You don't need to count feet.** You need to read aloud. If a sentence stumbles in the mouth, the rhythm is wrong. If it flows, the rhythm is right. Trust the ear over the theory.

**Cadence** — how a sentence ends. The final few syllables determine how the sentence "lands." End on a stressed syllable for finality. End on an unstressed syllable for openness, continuation. A strong cadence feels inevitable. A weak cadence feels like the sentence trailed off.

**Matching rhythm to emotion:**
- Tension: staccato, plosive-heavy, short stressed syllables
- Peace: flowing, liquid consonants, long open vowels
- Grief: monosyllabic, heavy, spondaic
- Joy: anapestic, light, bouncing
- Authority: periodic, measured, balanced stresses
