# Figurative Language

How words mean something other than their literal meaning. The engine of imaginative prose.

---

## Comparison

**Metaphor** — X IS Y. "Juliet is the sun." The comparison is implicit — no "like" or "as." Metaphor doesn't describe; it transforms. The reader sees one thing AS another, and both change in the seeing.

**Simile** — X is LIKE Y. "Her mind was like a butterfly farm in a hurricane." Explicit comparison. Simile keeps the two things separate — the reader sees the gap between them. Use simile when the distance between tenor and vehicle is part of the point.

**Extended metaphor / Conceit** — A single metaphor sustained across a passage or work. Donne comparing lovers' souls to the two feet of a compass. The metaphor isn't decoration — it's the structural spine. Every detail extends the comparison.

**Dead metaphor** — A metaphor so common it's no longer felt as figurative. "Leg of the table." "Foot of the mountain." Know they exist so you can either avoid them (for freshness) or deliberately revive them (by treating the literal meaning seriously).

**Mixed metaphor** — Two incompatible metaphors colliding. "We'll burn that bridge when we come to it." Usually an error. Occasionally brilliant for humor or to show a character's confused thinking.

**Analogy** — Extended comparison for explanation rather than beauty. More common in nonfiction and expository prose. "A skill is like training wheels for a bicycle, except the bicycle already knows how to ride."

## Personification & Attribution

**Personification** — Human qualities given to non-human things. "The gray-eyed morn smiles on the frowning night." Animate the world. Make it responsive. The environment becomes a character.

**Pathetic fallacy** — Nature reflecting a character's emotions. Storm during rage. Sunshine at the wedding. Beautiful when subtle, cliché when obvious. The strongest use is inverted — beautiful weather during grief, calm nature during violence.

**Prosopopoeia** — Giving voice to the absent, dead, or imaginary. A letter from a dead father. A speech from a personified abstraction. More specific than personification — the absent thing SPEAKS.

**Anthropomorphism** — Human characteristics to animals or gods who are characters. Pinocchio. The Cheshire Cat. Goes further than personification — the non-human thing becomes a person with interiority.

**Zoomorphism** — Animal characteristics applied to humans. She prowled. He snarled. Often reveals something predatory, instinctual, or subhuman about a character.

## Substitution

**Metonymy** — Naming something by an associated thing. "The Crown" for the monarchy. "The pen is mightier than the sword" — pen for writing, sword for military force. Creates compression and connotation.

**Synecdoche** — Part for whole, or whole for part. "All hands on deck." "Nice wheels." The specific part chosen reveals what matters — "hands" because the work matters, "wheels" because the car is about motion.

**Kenning** — Compound word as metonym. "Whale-road" for sea. "Sky-candle" for sun. Old English and Norse device. Creates a riddle-quality — the reader solves the image.

**Antonomasia** — Proper name used as general term, or phrase used as name. "The Bard" for Shakespeare. "He's a real Scrooge." Compresses an entire character into a reference.

**Euphemism** — Milder word for harsh reality. "Passed away" for died. "Let go" for fired. Reveals cultural discomfort, character politeness, or evasion.

**Dysphemism** — Harsher word than necessary. The opposite of euphemism. Reveals anger, contempt, or brutal honesty.

## Scale

**Hyperbole** — Exaggeration for emphasis or humor. "I've told you a million times." The exaggeration is not meant to deceive — it's meant to convey intensity.

**Understatement / Meiosis** — Deliberately understating. "The war situation has developed not necessarily to Japan's advantage." The gap between what's said and what's real IS the meaning.

**Litotes** — Understatement through negation. "Not bad" meaning "very good." "He was not unfamiliar with the bottle." The double negative creates a kind of reluctant admission.

---

## Irony

**Verbal irony** — Saying the opposite of what you mean. "Brutus is an honorable man." Requires trust that the audience will hear the gap. The unsaid meaning lives in the space between the words and their opposite.

**Dramatic irony** — The audience knows something the character doesn't. Othello trusts Iago. Oedipus searches for the murderer. Creates unbearable tension — we want to shout a warning through the page.

**Situational irony** — The outcome is the opposite of expectation. The fire station burns down. O. Henry's "Gift of the Magi." The universe's punchline.

**Cosmic irony** — Fate seems actively hostile. Oedipus fulfills the prophecy by trying to escape it. The universe is not just indifferent but cruelly structured against the character.

---

## Contradiction & Paradox

**Oxymoron** — Two-word paradox. "Terrible beauty." "Deafening silence." "Living death." The collision of opposites creates a meaning neither word carries alone.

**Paradox** — Apparently contradictory statement revealing truth. "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times." The contradiction is the point — reality is more complex than single statements allow.

---

## Wordplay & Surprise

**Zeugma** — One verb governing two objects in different senses. "He caught the train and a bad cold." The unexpected second meaning creates a comic or revelatory snap.

**Paraprosdokian** — Sentence that twists at the end. "I haven't slept for ten days, because that would be too long." The ending subverts the setup. Essential for humor.

**Anthimeria** — Using one word class as another. Verbing a noun. "Google it." "She porched the morning away." Creates freshness, voice, energy.

**Synesthesia** — One sense described in terms of another. "Loud colors." "Warm voice." "Bitter cold." The cross-wired senses create a richer, stranger perception.

---

## Symbolic & Allusive

**Symbolism** — Objects or settings carrying abstract meaning beyond their literal function. The green light in Gatsby. The scarlet letter. Symbols should emerge from the story, not be planted from outside.

**Allegory** — The entire story functions as extended metaphor. Animal Farm = Russian Revolution. Every literal element maps to an abstract one.

**Allusion** — Reference to another work, person, event, or myth. Creates resonance — the reader who catches the allusion gets a second layer of meaning.

**Intertextuality** — Broader than allusion. Conscious embedding of other texts — their structures, tropes, language — within yours. The text is in conversation with other texts.

**Objective correlative** — (T.S. Eliot) Expressing emotion through objects, situations, or events rather than naming the emotion. The untouched dinner. The ticking clock. The wilting flowers. The reader feels the emotion without being told what to feel.
