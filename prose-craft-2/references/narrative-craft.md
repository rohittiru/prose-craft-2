# Narrative Craft

Point of view, psychic distance, show vs tell, scene construction — and the macro-structural choices (time order, multiplicity, moral framing) that most separate human from machine-default fiction.

---

## Psychic Distance

How close or far the reader feels from the character's consciousness. The zoom lens of fiction.

**Level 1: Panoramic** — Maximum distance. "It was a dark time in the history of the city." The reader is a satellite. No character, no specificity. Use for establishing context, opening wide, or creating a sense of historical sweep.

**Level 2: Biographical** — Narrator summarizing. "Sarah had always been a quiet woman." The reader knows ABOUT the character but isn't WITH them. Use for backstory, transition, or characterization in summary.

**Level 3: Close narration** — Following the character in the moment. "Sarah walked into the room and noticed the broken vase." The reader is behind the character's shoulder. The camera is close but the narrator's voice is still distinct from the character's.

**Level 4: Deep limited** — The character's perceptions color the narration. "The room smelled of old wood and something sour, like milk left too long." The reader sees through the character's senses. The narrator and character are nearly fused.

**Level 5: Interior** — Inside unfiltered thought. "God, not again. Not today. She couldn't do this today." The reader IS the character. Grammar loosens. Syntax follows the mind, not the rules.

**The key craft skill is MOVEMENT between levels.** Great prose zooms in and out — opening wide for context, then diving deep for an emotional moment, then pulling back to breathe. Staying at one level for too long creates monotony. The shifts themselves create energy.

**Free indirect discourse** — Third person narration that takes on the character's voice, vocabulary, and thought patterns without switching to first person or using "she thought." "She walked to the window. The garden was a mess. Of course it was. When had anything in this house not been a mess." This is the character's voice running through the narrator's grammar. The most important innovation in modern fiction prose. Austen invented it. Flaubert perfected it. Joyce, Woolf, and everyone after them depends on it.

**Filter words** — "She saw," "he felt," "she noticed," "he realized." These increase distance by reminding the reader that a character is perceiving rather than letting the reader perceive directly. "She noticed the curtain moving" is more distant than "The curtain moved." Eliminate filter words to close psychic distance.

## Show vs Tell

**Showing** = dramatization. Concrete, sensory, present-tense-feeling. The reader experiences the moment. "Her knuckles whitened around the coffee mug."

**Telling** = summary, statement, explanation. The narrator informs. "Sarah was furious about the meeting."

**The rule isn't "always show."** The rule is: show the important moments, tell the transitions. Scene for drama; summary for connective tissue. A novel that shows everything is exhausting. A novel that tells everything is a Wikipedia plot summary.

> **Calibration note.** "Always show, never tell" is part of what pushes Claude onto the machine center — the relentlessly embodied, sensory, never-named register. Humans *tell* emotion more than AI does (they name feelings 29% vs 8%). Telling is not the amateur move it's often made out to be; the reflexive, total avoidance of it is its own tell. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 2.

**Concrete detail** — The building block of showing. Specific, tangible, sensory. Not "a tree" but "an elm." Not "he walked" but "he shuffled." Not "she was sad" but "she pressed her thumbnail into the tabletop until the wood creaked."

**The telling detail** — A single selected detail that reveals more than a paragraph of description. Not everything in the room — ONE object that tells the reader who lives here, what happened, what kind of world this is.

**Objective correlative** — Express emotion through things, not names. The half-eaten sandwich still on the counter three days later. The second coffee mug, washed and put away, unused for months. *Powerful — and over-used by AI.* Setting-as-emotional-mirror is a measured machine tell. Use the objective correlative as one tool among several, and sometimes let the prose just name the feeling instead.

**Subtext** — What is implied but never stated. The reader fills the gap. Characters who talk around the real subject. A scene whose surface meaning differs from its emotional meaning. Subtext trusts the reader to be intelligent.

## Point of View

**First person** — "I." Maximum intimacy, maximum restriction. The reader knows only what the narrator knows. Unreliable narration is built into the form.

**Third person limited** — Following one character's perspective in third person. The workhorse of modern fiction. Close enough for intimacy, flexible enough for zooming.

**Third person omniscient** — The narrator knows everything, sees everything, can enter any mind. Requires a strong narrative voice to avoid feeling diffuse. (An omniscient narrator with personality is also a natural home for the reader-address dial — see below.)

**Second person** — "You." Rare. Creates an unusual intimacy or accusation. Calvino, McInerney, Adichie.

**Unreliable narrator** — The account cannot be fully trusted — lying, deluded, naive, unstable. The reader reads against the narration to find the truth.

**Multiperspectivity** — Multiple POV characters, each revealing different facets. Creates complexity, but each voice must be distinct.

## Temporal Architecture (the macro time-order of the story)

Distinct from sentence rhythm — this is the *order in which the story delivers its events*, and it's one of the strongest human-vs-AI separators. AI defaults to straight chronology, first clue to final reveal. Humans fracture, withhold, and double back far more.

**Linear / chronological** — Events in the order they happen. Clean, propulsive, sometimes the right call (real-time unity-of-action pieces are stronger linear). But it is also the machine default — choose it on purpose, not by reflex.

**Analepsis (flashback)** — A scene from before the current timeline. Most effective when *triggered* by something in the present — a smell, a word, an object — rather than announced ("Years earlier…").

**Prolepsis (flash-forward)** — A leap ahead, often to seed dread or inevitability. A single early flash-forward can recolor everything that follows.

**Delayed disclosure** — Withholding a key fact the narrator *knows*, so its later arrival reframes what came before. The reader passes the evidence without registering it.

**Recontextualizing revelation** — The reveal that makes the reader mentally *reread* earlier scenes. Humans do this markedly more than AI. The craft is in planting the detail early, in plain sight, that the reveal later re-lights.

**Anachrony / nonlinear structure** — A timeline deliberately out of order: a story that opens at the funeral and spirals backward through decades. Use when the *meaning* lives in the juxtaposition of times, not the sequence of events.

**Iterative / habitual narration** — "Every Sunday she would…" Compressing repeated events into one. A quietly human texture AI tends to skip in favor of singular scenes.

**How to deploy:** decide the time strategy at the Architecture stage, before writing. Don't fracture a timeline for its own sake — fracture it when a buried cause, a secret, or a reveal *wants* the reader to arrive out of order. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 1.

## Structural Multiplicity (subplots, locations, ensemble)

AI strongly favors a single tidy track; humans run more threads and tie them together. The human signal is not *more stuff* — it's *integrated* multiplicity.

**Subplots** — A secondary thread that **echoes or complicates** the central theme (thematically parallel, or pointedly contrasting). A subplot that wanders off is worse than none; a subplot that rhymes with the main line deepens it.

**Multiple locations** — Moving the story across distinct physical spaces changes register and widens the world. AI tends to stay put.

**Ensemble / social network** — More named characters in genuine relation, talk that ripples through a community (gossip, rumor, reputation). A richer social web is a human marker.

**External resolution** — Let the world act on the protagonist: coincidence, another person's choice, outside force. AI over-resolves through the protagonist's *internal* growth/acceptance; reality is messier and more various.

**Caveat (weight softly):** this multiplicity cluster is the one most plausibly inflated by the study's setup (AI wrote to a fixed brief and word count). Treat it as a real option, not a quota. A miniature wants one clean track. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 6.

## Moral Framing of the Protagonist

Humans present morally ambivalent protagonists much more often (59% vs 38%); AI leans clearly-sympathetic and redeems on schedule.

**Ambivalence** — Let the character be genuinely hard to root for: selfish without being cute, complicit, self-deceiving, cruel without full justification. The reader's sympathy should be *uneasy*.

**Withhold the verdict** — Don't have the prose or other characters reassure the reader that the protagonist is good underneath. Refuse the tidy redemption arc; let them end about as compromised as they began.

**When clarity is right** — Children's POV, fable, certain comedy want moral clarity. The point is range: don't let clean-and-sympathetic be the only protagonist you write. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 7.

## Story & Scene Construction

**In medias res** — Starting in the middle of action. The reader is dropped into events already in motion and pieces context together.

**Foreshadowing** — Planting details early that pay off later. Effective foreshadowing is invisible on first reading and obvious on second.

**Chekhov's gun** — Every detail must be necessary. A gun on the wall in act one must fire by act three; a gun that fires in act three should have been on the wall in act one.

**Scene vs summary** — Scene = real-time dramatization with dialogue, action, sensory detail. Summary = compressed time, telling, transition. The shift between them IS pacing.

**Dramatic irony** — The reader knows something the character doesn't. Tension, dread, or comedy depending on what's withheld.

**Metafiction & the fourth wall** — Fiction that knows it's fiction: the narrator commenting on the act of narration, an aside to the reader, the story acknowledging its own making. v1 treated this as ego-display to suppress. The data says deliberate reader-address and fourth-wall work are strongly *human*. Distinguish **performance** (winking to look clever — cut) from **a narrator who genuinely addresses you** (a legitimate, distinctively human voice). Deploy as a committed posture, varied across pieces, never as a constant tic. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 3.

**Iceberg theory** — What you leave out strengthens what remains. "If a writer of prose knows enough about what he is writing about he may omit things that he knows and the reader… will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them."

**Deliberate ambiguity** — Multiple valid interpretations left unresolved. Kafka, Carver, Ishiguro. *Note:* the open, unresolved, quiet ending is also a Claude default. Ambiguity is a real tool — but vary it with endings that actually close, sometimes loudly. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 5.
