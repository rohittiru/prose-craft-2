# Repetition & Sentence Architecture

How repeating words and shaping sentences creates emphasis, rhythm, and meaning.

---

## Repetition by Position

**Anaphora** — Same words at the BEGINNING of successive clauses. The workhorse of emotional momentum. "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields." Use when you want to build, accumulate, drive forward relentlessly. Each repetition adds weight.

**Epistrophe** — Same words at the END. "Of the people, by the people, for the people." Creates a hammering, landing quality — each clause arriving at the same destination by different paths.

**Symploce** — Anaphora + Epistrophe combined — different words repeating at beginning AND end. "I'm willing to tell you. I'm wanting to tell you. I'm waiting to tell you." Double reinforcement. Use rarely — it's powerful but can feel mechanical.

**Epanalepsis** — Same word at beginning and end of a single clause. "Once more unto the breach, dear friends, once more!" Creates a circular, enclosing feeling.

**Anadiplosis** — The last word of one clause becomes the first word of the next. "To die, to sleep; / To sleep, perchance to dream." Creates chain-link momentum — each sentence handed off to the next.

**Mesodiplosis** — Repetition in the MIDDLE of successive clauses. Subtle, often barely noticed. "Troubled on every side, yet not distressed; perplexed, but not in despair."

**Conduplicatio** — A key word repeated throughout a passage, not in a fixed position. Thematic threading. The word becomes a motif within the paragraph.

---

## Repetition by Method

**Epizeuxis** — Same word with NO intervening words. "Horror! Horror! Horror!" Raw emotional force. Use at moments of extreme feeling where articulate speech breaks down.

**Diacope** — Same word with one or two words between. "A horse! A horse! My kingdom for a horse!" Slightly more controlled than epizeuxis but still urgent.

**Polyptoton** — Same ROOT word in different grammatical forms. "Diamond me no diamonds, prize me no prizes." Creates a playful, self-referential quality.

**Antanaclasis** — Same word repeated but with DIFFERENT MEANING. "We must all hang together, or we shall all hang separately." The shift in meaning IS the wit.

## Sentence Architecture

### Parallel and Reversed Structures

**Parallelism** — Similar grammatical structures in successive clauses. The foundation of most rhetorical power. Without parallelism, repetition devices don't work — the repeated shape IS the music.

**Isocolon** — Parallel structures of EQUAL LENGTH. Creates balance, formality, a sense of law or permanence.

**Tricolon** — Three parallel elements. The magic number. Two feels incomplete. Four feels like a list. Three feels like truth. "Veni, vidi, vici."

**Antithesis** — Opposite ideas in parallel structure. "Some rise by sin, and some by virtue fall." The parallel form makes the opposition sharp. Without the structural echo, it's just two different ideas.

**Chiasmus** — Parallel structure but REVERSED in the second part. "Who dotes, yet doubts; suspects, yet strongly loves." The cross-pattern creates a sense of complexity, entanglement.

**Antimetabole** — Exact same words in reversed order. "Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country." The reversal IS the argument. The form makes the point.

### Addition and Omission

**Asyndeton** — Removing conjunctions. "Accursed, unhappy, wretched, hateful day!" Creates speed, urgency, breathlessness. Each word hits without cushioning.

**Polysyndeton** — Adding MORE conjunctions than needed. "And pray, and sing, and tell old tales, and laugh." Creates a sense of abundance, overflow, liturgical accumulation. Slows the reader down and makes them take each item separately.

**Auxesis / Climax** — Arranging from least to most significant. Building intensity. Each item more powerful than the last.

**Anticlimax / Bathos** — Arranging from most to least significant. Deflation. Often for humor — the grand statement followed by the mundane.

**Amplification** — Repeating while adding detail. Expanding a point for emphasis. "Not just tired — exhausted, wrung out, emptied of everything but the ache."

### Sentence Types as Craft Choices

**Periodic sentence** — Main clause WITHHELD until the end. "In spite of the rain, the cold, the cancellation of every train, the fact that she had no umbrella and her shoes had holes — she came." Builds suspense through syntax. The meaning is suspended until the final words. Creates a sense of inevitability at the landing.

**Cumulative / Loose sentence** — Main clause FIRST, then details unfurling. "He walked down the street, a solitary figure, shoulders slumped, gaze fixed on the pavement, oblivious to everything." Mimics the mind observing. The core fact arrives early; the details are discovered as the eye moves.

**Fragment** — Deliberately incomplete. "Gone." "Nothing." "Not a sound." Maximum impact, minimum words. Use at moments of emotional intensity or sharp transition.

**Run-on (deliberate)** — Fused without proper punctuation. Creates breathlessness, overwhelm, the sense of a mind racing beyond the capacity of syntax to contain it. Faulkner, Saramago. Use with intention — accidental run-ons are errors; deliberate ones are style.

### Word Order

**Anastrophe** — Inverted word order. "Strong you are." Creates emphasis on the displaced word and a sense of formality or strangeness.

**Hyperbaton** — Separating two words that normally go together. Creates suspense — the reader waits for the completion.

**Tmesis** — Inserting a word into a compound word. "Un-bloody-believable." Voice. Energy. Informality.

**Aposiopesis** — Breaking off mid-sentence. The dash, the ellipsis, the unfinished thought. Creates drama, emotion too strong for words, or deliberate refusal to say more.

**Caesura** — A strong pause in the middle of a line or sentence. "Know then thyself. Presume not God to scan." The pause forces the reader to absorb the first half before continuing.
