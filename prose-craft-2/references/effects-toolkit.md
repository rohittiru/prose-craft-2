# Effects Toolkit

This file is organized by **what you want the prose to DO** — not by device taxonomy. Each effect lists the techniques that create it, why they work together, and what to avoid.

> **Calibration note (read once).** Several recipes below — especially Beauty, Grief, and Intimacy — lean on moves that are *also* measured AI signatures: embodied-only emotion, heavy sensory layering, setting-as-mirror, the quiet restrained close. They are not wrong. But if you reach for the same lush-and-quiet palette on every emotional moment, you land on the machine center. Where a recipe is marked **⚖ machine-leaning**, treat its palette as one option, check `human-signal.md` for the divergent move, and remember the Posture Board: per piece commit, across pieces vary.

## Create Tension / Dread

**Reach for:**
- Short sentences. Fragments. The period is a held breath.
- Asyndeton — strip conjunctions. "She ran. Fell. Got up. Ran again."
- Close psychic distance — deep inside the character's fear
- Cacophony — hard consonants (k, t, d, p, b), hissing sounds
- Concrete sensory detail — the specific sound, the specific smell. Not "she was afraid" but "the floorboard under her left foot gave half an inch"
- White space — short paragraphs surrounded by emptiness
- Foreshadowing — plant the detail early that pays off in the moment of dread
- Dramatic irony — the reader knows the danger before the character does
- One-sentence paragraphs at the peak moment

**Avoid:**
- Long, flowing sentences (they relax the reader)
- Explaining the emotion ("she felt a chill of terror run down her spine")
- Latinate vocabulary (too formal, too distant)

> ⚖ **Calibration:** v1 also said "avoid metaphor / never name the feeling." Naming fear flatly — "She was afraid. She went in anyway." — can cut harder than another somatic clause, and it's a human-leaning move (see `human-signal.md`, Dial 2). And not all dread is close-and-embodied: the long, controlled, eerily calm sentence during a scene that should be chaos is its own kind of unease.

## Create Beauty / Lyricism  ⚖ machine-leaning

**Reach for:**
- Cumulative/loose sentences — the main clause first, then details unfurling like cloth
- Euphony — liquid consonants (l, m, n, r), long vowels, open syllables
- Imagery — sight, sound, texture. Layer the senses
- Simile and metaphor — this is where figurative language earns its keep
- Assonance — repeating vowel sounds creates a singing quality
- Polysyndeton — "and" repeated, creating a liturgical, additive feeling
- Longer paragraphs — let the reader sink in
- Medium psychic distance — observing beauty, not drowning in it
- Cadence — land sentence endings on a stressed syllable, on a strong word

**Avoid:**
- Purple prose — the line between lyrical and overwritten is "this image earns its place" vs "this image exists to show off"
- Stacking adjectives — one precise adjective beats three vague ones
- Euphony without content — beautiful sounds wrapping an empty sentence

> ⚖ **Calibration:** lush sensory beauty is the machine's most over-produced register (high sensory density, smell-heavy, setting-as-mirror are all AI tells). Beauty is still a legitimate aim — but make it a *chosen* register, not the default for every tender moment. Sometimes spare, plain statement is more beautiful than lavish description. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 8.

## Build Emotional Momentum / Crescendo

**Reach for:**
- Anaphora — repeating the opening phrase drives forward like a drumbeat. "She remembered…" "She remembered…"
- Parallel structure — matching clause shapes creates accumulation
- Auxesis/Climax — each item in the series more intense than the last
- Periodic sentences — withholding the main clause until the end, building suspense through syntax
- Polysyndeton — "and" piled up creates a sense of overflow, things spilling beyond control
- Rising rhythm — iambic patterns (da-DUM) feel like climbing
- Gradually increasing sentence length — each sentence slightly longer, building wave energy
- Close psychic distance, getting closer as momentum builds

**Avoid:**
- Breaking the momentum with explanation or qualification
- Deflating with humor (unless that's the intent — bathos is a device, not a mistake)
- Shifting psychic distance outward in the middle of the build

> **Note:** This effect is the antidote to Claude's #1 fingerprint — *flat event escalation*. Use it deliberately. Let stakes compound; let an ending actually crest and break rather than fade. See `human-signal.md`, Dial 5.

## Reveal Character Without Stating It

**Reach for:**
- Telling detail — the ONE object, gesture, or choice that reveals who this person is. Not "she was meticulous" but "she aligned the pens on her desk by height"
- Free indirect discourse — third person narration colored by the character's diction and thought patterns
- Objective correlative — objects reflecting inner state. The untouched coffee. The unmade bed
- Dialogue subtext — what they DON'T say
- Speech rhythm — clipped for the controlled; run-on for the unraveling
- Action beats — what they do with hands, eyes, body while talking
- Diction shift — the words a character uses (or avoids) reveal education, class, region, era

**Avoid:**
- Direct statement of personality traits
- Having other characters describe them ("You've always been so brave")
- Internal monologue that explains the character's own nature

> **Calibration:** revealing character is also a chance to make them *morally ambivalent* rather than cleanly sympathetic — a human-leaning move Claude underuses (see `human-signal.md`, Dial 7). The telling detail can incriminate as easily as endear.

## Slow the Reader Down

**Reach for:**
- Long sentences — cumulative, winding, clause after clause
- Polysyndeton — the repeated "and" forces the reader to take each item separately
- Sensory detail — layer the senses
- Periodic sentences — the meaning delayed, the reader waiting
- Deep psychic distance — inside the character's perceptions
- Scene (real-time dramatization) rather than summary
- Longer paragraphs with few breaks
- Latinate diction — longer words take longer to read
- Ekphrasis — detailed description of a visual scene or object

**Avoid:**
- Fragments (they speed up)
- Asyndeton (it accelerates)
- Short paragraphs (they create urgency)

## Speed the Reader Up

**Reach for:**
- Short sentences. Fragments. Even single words as paragraphs
- Asyndeton — no conjunctions, just action after action
- Anglo-Saxon diction — one-syllable, blunt words. Hit. Run. Fall. Break
- White space — short paragraphs, lots of breaks
- Summary rather than scene — compress time. "Three days passed"
- Hard cuts — jump between moments without transition
- Active voice, strong verbs, no adverbs
- Dialogue without beats or tags — just the voices, rapid-fire
- Close psychic distance in action, far distance in summary

**Avoid:**
- Long descriptive passages
- Interior monologue during action
- Polysyndeton (it slows)

## Make the Reader Laugh

**Reach for:**
- Incongruity — the unexpected collision of mismatched things
- Paraprosdokian — the sentence that twists at the end
- Deadpan/Understatement — delivering the absurd in flat, emotionless prose. The gap between content and tone IS the joke
- Rule of three — setup, setup, subversion. "She packed her books, her passport, and a profound sense of regret"
- Bathos — sudden descent from elevated to mundane. "He contemplated the infinite majesty of the cosmos. His sandwich was stale"
- Specific detail — comedy lives in specificity. Not "a dog" but "a three-legged Pomeranian named Steve"
- Comic timing — the pause before the punchline. In prose, the paragraph break or the comma before the twist
- Irony (verbal) — saying the opposite of what you mean
- Gossip and banter — comedy often lives in how people talk *about* each other (a human dialogue texture; see `human-signal.md`, Dial 9)

**Avoid:**
- Explaining the joke
- Signaling that something is funny ("hilariously," "comically")
- Landing on wisdom or reflection at the end of a funny piece — the last line is a joke, not a lesson
- Self-deprecation that turns earnest

## Create Unease / The Uncanny

**Reach for:**
- Defamiliarization — describe something ordinary as if seeing it for the first time
- Mixed register — formal diction applied to violent or domestic content. Clinical language for emotional events
- Unreliable narration — the narrator's calm doesn't match the horror described
- Deliberate ambiguity — multiple interpretations left unresolved
- Cacophony — sounds that grate, hiss, clash
- Pathetic fallacy inverted — nature too calm, too beautiful, while something terrible happens
- Long, controlled sentences during moments that should be chaotic — the control IS the unease
- Specific, wrong details — the thing that doesn't belong in the scene

**Avoid:**
- Naming the emotion ("he felt uneasy")
- Jump scares (they work in film, not prose)
- Over-describing the source — let the reader's imagination do the worst work

## Create Grief / Loss / Elegy  ⚖ machine-leaning

**Reach for:**
- Short, concrete sentences. Grief simplifies language. "She sat down. She didn't get up for a long time"
- Anglo-Saxon diction — blunt, monosyllabic, physical words
- Close psychic distance — inside the body, the disorientation
- Silence — what's not said. Dialogue that avoids the subject
- Anaphora — "She would never…" repeated, each iteration hitting harder
- Sentence fragments — thought breaking apart
- Present tense — grief has no past tense; it's always happening now
- White space — gaps on the page mirroring gaps in the person

**Avoid:**
- Consolation within the piece — don't comfort the reader. Let the grief sit
- Sentimentality — sentiment is earned, sentimentality is not

> ⚖ **Calibration — this recipe is the densest cluster of AI tells.** v1 said: render grief through the *objective correlative* (the coat on the hook, the half-finished crossword), *avoid naming the emotion*, *avoid metaphor*, keep it quiet. That is almost exactly the measured machine profile — embodied-only emotion, setting-as-mirror, restrained close. Grief is real and these tools work, but vary the pole: humans *name* grief plainly far more than AI ("She was not all right, and would not be for a long time"). And grief is not always quiet — it can rage, escalate, break things. Let some elegies get loud. See `human-signal.md`, Dials 2 and 5.

## Create Intimacy / Closeness  ⚖ machine-leaning

**Reach for:**
- Free indirect discourse — narrator's voice and character's voice merging
- Deep psychic distance — Level 4 or 5, inside the character's perceptions
- Second person (sparingly) — "you" puts the reader in the scene
- Sensory detail that's personal, bodily — warmth, texture, the feel of fabric or skin
- Present tense — immediacy
- Simple diction — intimate moments use small words
- Short paragraphs — the reader and character alone together
- Dialogue without tags — two voices, close enough not to need attribution

**Avoid:**
- Omniscient narration (too distant)
- Explaining the significance of the moment
- Long descriptive passages (they observe rather than inhabit)

> ⚖ **Calibration:** the "personal bodily sensory detail" reflex is, again, embodied-emotion-heavy machine territory. Intimacy can also come from a narrator who *addresses you directly* — the confiding "you," the aside that lets the reader in — which is a strongly human move v1 suppressed. See `human-signal.md`, Dials 2 and 3.

## Create Authority / Weight / Gravitas

**Reach for:**
- Periodic sentences — withholding the main clause creates earned arrival
- Parallel structure — balanced, deliberate, symmetrical
- Tricolon — three-part structures ("of the people, by the people, for the people")
- Isocolon — phrases of equal length, a sense of law, permanence
- Spondee — consecutive stressed syllables. Heavy. Slow. Certain
- Aphorism — a single sentence that sounds carved in stone
- Short paragraphs — each point standing alone
- Latinate diction — longer words carry institutional weight
- Chiasmus — the reversed structure feels like truth

**Avoid:**
- Hedging, qualifying, softening
- Humor (it undercuts authority unless you're very good)
- Questions (they admit uncertainty)
